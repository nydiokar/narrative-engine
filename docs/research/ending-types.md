# Ending Types

The ending sanctions the entire narrative — it is the final actantial junction state. The ending type determines whether the canonical schema's sanction phase is fulfilled.

## Ending Types

| Type | Description | Typical Emotional Effect | Typical Genre Fit |
|------|-------------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **Restoration** | Order is restored; equilibrium returns | Satisfied, resolved | Fantasy, adventure, comedy |
| **Transformation** | A new and better order is established | Hopeful, uplifted | Coming-of-age, fantasy, literary |
| **Tragic** | The Subject fails; the Object is lost forever | Grief, catharsis | Tragedy, literary |
| **Bittersweet** | Gain with significant loss; mixed outcome | Melancholic acceptance | Literary, historical, drama |
| **Pyrrhic Victory** | The Object is attained but at devastating cost | Troubled, ambivalent | Tragedy, war, thriller |
| **Ambiguous** | The outcome is deliberately unclear | Unsettled, thoughtful | Literary, mystery, psychological |
| **Circular** | The ending mirrors the beginning; nothing fundamental changed | Ironic, resigned | Literary, satire |
| **Open** | The central conflict is resolved but new questions remain | Curious, expectant | Series, sci-fi, fantasy |
| **Revelatory** | A final truth recontextualises everything | Astonished, re-evaluative | Mystery, thriller, literary |
| **Ironic** | The outcome contradicts the Subject's expectations | Wry, thoughtful | Satire, literary, crime |
| **Redemptive** | The Subject achieves forgiveness or moral restoration | Hopeful, cathartic | Literary, drama, western |
| **Corruption** | The Subject succumbs to the dark outcome | Disturbed, warned | Crime, tragedy, horror |
| **Sacrificial** | The Subject gives up their life or core value for a greater good | Noble grief, admiration | Tragedy, fantasy, war |
| **False Happy** | Appears happy but the reader knows the truth is darker | Ironic discomfort | Thriller, literary, satire |
| **Cosmic Horror** | The Subject confronts an indifferent or incomprehensible reality | Dread, insignificance | Horror, weird fiction |
| **Romantic Union** | The lovers are united | Joyful, satisfied | Romance, romantic comedy |
| **Romantic Separation** | The lovers part, by choice or fate | Sad, poignant | Romance, tragedy, literary |
| **Coming Home** | The Subject returns to their origin transformed | Wistful, resolved | Literary, drama, fantasy |
| **Exile** | The Subject is cast out, alone | Lonely, unresolved | Tragedy, literary |
| **Ascension** | The Subject rises to a higher state or station | Triumphant, elevated | Fantasy, mythic, epic |
| **Fall** | The Subject descends to a lower state | Somber, cautionary | Tragedy, crime, horror |

## Ending Compatibility

The ending type must be compatible with:

1. **The Object of Value** — if the Object was Justice, a Tragic ending may undermine thematic coherence (unless the point is that justice is impossible).
2. **The Genre Promise** — Romance readers expect Romantic Union or Sacrificial, not Cosmic Horror.
3. **The Canonical Schema** — the Sanction phase must complete. The ending is the final sanction.
4. **The Subject's Arc** — the ending must be a plausible outcome of the Subject's choices and modality states.

## Ending as Sanction

The Critic evaluates the ending against:
- Does it follow causally from the Performance phase?
- Does it answer the moral question posed by the theme?
- Does it respect the genre contract?
- Does it transform the reader's understanding of the preceding events?
